## 🧠 AI Research & Papers

**[OpenAI's CoT-Control study](https://openai.com/index/reasoning-models-chain-of-thought-controllability)** is the most technically interesting safety paper of the week. The finding: reasoning models struggle to deliberately manipulate their visible chains of thought — they can't easily think one thing and show another. This reinforces chain-of-thought as a *genuine* monitoring surface, not just theater. The practical implication is that CoT monitoring is a more reliable safety layer than previously assumed, which matters for anyone building agentic systems with oversight requirements.

**[Sebastian Raschka on LLM evaluation approaches](https://magazine.sebastianraschka.com/p/llm-evaluation-4-approaches)** provides a practitioner-grade breakdown of the four main eval paradigms: multiple-choice benchmarks, verifiers, leaderboards, and LLM judges. As benchmarks saturate (AI is [acing math exams faster than scientists write them](https://spectrum.ieee.org/ai-math-benchmarks)), the community is being forced toward LLM-as-judge and domain-specific verifiers. This connects to LangChain's release of community-driven evals (see Tools section).

**[Raschka on inference-time scaling](https://magazine.sebastianraschka.com/p/categories-of-inference-time-scaling)** surveys the emerging taxonomy of test-time compute techniques — best-of-N sampling, process reward models, tree search variants. This is the most actively researched area in practical LLM deployment right now, as it's the primary lever labs are pulling post-pretraining.

**LLM de-anonymization**: Two converging findings this week — [Ars Technica covers a study](https://arstechnica.com/security/2026/03/llms-can-unmask-pseudonymous-users-at-scale-with-surprising-accuracy/) showing LLMs can unmask pseudonymous users at scale with surprising accuracy, and [The Guardian reports AI is enabling hackers to identify anonymous social media accounts](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMikwFBVV95cUxOS3pOcTBKMldPRlFpZVRveXdaOHk1bEJ3eGFZczBvSno5eHB4VURVaUlqOFZ3WlZHaVVUemZ2VG5oaDBWOUY1ZFVYU0R1X2FFbXVQandjRHFQdFZjemFmWE5TRkFORXVTbUhlcWVuZ1A5eEpqelhCQ1RBMnEtd3Q2aWpPb09ybDZ4NFlHVEs0SUp1VUE). Pseudonymity as a privacy strategy is functionally collapsing. This has direct implications for threat modeling in any application handling user-generated content.
